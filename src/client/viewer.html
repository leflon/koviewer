South Korea administrative divisions

Upper-level local autonomy (시도)

시도 (Sido) represents the highest tier of local government in South Korea, functioning as the primary administrative division below the central government. This level possesses significant autonomy and is legally distinct as a broad-scale regional authority. Administratively, it is responsible for handling affairs that span wide geographic areas, such as regional transportation planning, environmental conservation, and high-level education policies. Governance is democratic; residents directly elect the head of the local government and members of the local council every four years. The administrative center is usually a large complex, and citizens typically visit here only for major regulatory issues, passport services (though often delegated), or region-wide complaints, as day-to-day affairs are handled at lower levels.

도 (Do)

도 (Do) refers to a Province, which is the traditional regional unit covering large geographic territories with a mix of urban and rural populations. There are 9 of them, including 3 Special self-governing provinces. Legally, a Province is led by a Governor (Dojisa) who is elected by popular vote, alongside a Provincial Council that acts as the legislative check. Administratively, the provincial government manages the coordination between the central government and the smaller cities or counties within its jurisdiction. It focuses on balanced regional development, agriculture, and infrastructure that connects its various sub-divisions.

The South Korean administrative system also maintains a symbolic claim over northern territory through the 이북5도 (Ibuk-5-do), or the Five Northern Provinces. Managed by the 이북5도위원회 (Committee for the Five Northern Korean Provinces) , these entities are legally defined by the administrative boundaries of 1945 (ko), strictly ignoring changes made by the North Korean regime. As the South possesses no de facto control over this land, the honorary governors are formally appointed by the President of the Republic of Korea and their role is not territorial governance. Instead, their administrative mandate is centered on supporting the community of displaced families and North Korean defectors currently residing in the South, preserving their regional cultural heritage, and maintaining the legal framework for potential future reunification.

시 (Si)

시 (Si) at this level refers to Special Cities, Metropolitan Cities, or Special Self-Governing Cities, which are administratively equivalent to a Province but are distinctively urban. These entities, such as Seoul or Busan, are led by a Mayor (Sijang) rather than a Governor, but they possess the same legal standing and autonomy as a Province. They manage high-density urban planning, public transit systems like subways, and city-wide zoning. Residents elect the Mayor and City Council members directly, and the administration is tailored to the complexities of managing massive metropolitan populations independent of a surrounding province.

Lower-level local autonomy (시군구)

시군구 (Sigungu) serves as the basic local government unit and is the tier where most direct interaction between the government and the people occurs. Legally, this level has its own autonomous status (except for non-autonomous districts in certain cities) with independent budgets and powers delegated by the upper Sido level. Administratively, these units are responsible for the majority of civil registrations, such as birth and marriage certificates, local tax collection, land registration, and sanitation. Residents generally feel a stronger connection to this level of government for daily needs.

시 (Si)

시 (Si) at this tier is a specific administrative designation for a City that has a population typically over 50,000 and an urbanized structure, situated within a Province. It is led by an elected Mayor and monitored by a City Council. Legally, it operates independently from the county system and manages its own urban development, water supply, and local economic promotion. If the population exceeds 500,000, it may receive additional administrative powers similar to a metropolitan city, allowing it to manage its own districts, though it remains under the provincial jurisdiction.

군 (Gun)

군 (Gun) is a County, predominantly finding its place in rural areas or less urbanized regions within a Province or sometimes within a Metropolitan City. It is governed by an elected County Governor (Gunsu) and a County Council. Administratively, the focus here is heavily tilted toward agriculture, forestry, and rural development support. The population density is lower than in a Si, and the administrative services often include substantial support for farming communities and preserving local traditions and landscapes.

구 (Gu)

구 (Gu) is a District found strictly within larger cities. There is a critical legal distinction here: in Special or Metropolitan Cities (like Seoul), these are "Autonomous Districts" (Jachigu) where residents elect a District Mayor (Gucheongjang) and Council, possessing their own taxation powers and local autonomy. However, in large regular Cities, these are "Administrative Districts" (Ilbangu) which are merely non-autonomous branches of the city government used to improve administrative efficiency. In these cases, the head is an appointed official, not elected, and there is no district council. Residents visit the District Office for passport applications, parking permits, and social welfare registration.

Subordinate Administrative Level (읍면동)

읍면동 (Eupmyeondong) acts as the supplementary administrative unit comprising the sub-divisions of the Sigungu. This level is strictly administrative and legally non-autonomous, meaning there are no local elections held for these positions. The heads of these divisions are career civil servants appointed by the Mayor or County Governor. The primary function of this level is to act as the terminal administrative capillary, delivering welfare benefits, handling resident registration (move-in reports), and issuing various civil documents directly to the public.

읍 (Eup)

읍 (Eup) is a Town division, typically serving as the center of commerce and population within a rural County (Gun) or a localized urban center within a mixed City. To be designated as an Eup, the area usually needs a population of at least 20,000 and a distinct urbanized street grid. Administratively, the Eup office handles tasks similar to the Myeon but on a slightly larger scale due to the higher population density, often managing waste disposal coordination and local public facility maintenance.

면 (Myeon)

면 (Myeon) is a Township, representing the rural countryside within a County or City. It typically has a lower population density than an Eup and is composed primarily of farming or fishing communities. The Myeon office serves as the administrative hub for a wide geographic area of scattered villages. The appointed Head of Myeon focuses on agricultural support, disaster prevention in rural areas (like flood control), and coordinating social services for elderly residents who make up a large portion of the demographic.

동 (Dong)

동 (Dong) is a Neighborhood division found in urban Districts (Gu) or Cities (Si). It is the smallest urban administrative unit where the "Community Service Center" (often called the Happy Community Center) is located. This is the most frequented government office for urban residents to register residency, obtain seal certificates

Village level (리)

리 (Ri) is the Village unit, serving as the smallest subdivision of rural towns (Eup) and townships (Myeon). Unlike the urban Dong, the Ri is a natural village settlement. While not a fully autonomous level of government, it has a unique administrative role where the Village Head (Ijang) acts as a liaison. The Ijang is typically selected by the villagers (often via a village meeting or consensus) and formally appointed by the Eup/Myeon head. There is no government office building for a Ri; instead, the focus is on the "Village Hall" (Maeul Hoegwan), which acts as a communal gathering space for seniors and a venue for village meetings regarding agricultural cooperation or community events.
